# SECTION 1 ADDITIONS — NEW PATTERNS (P23–P37)

### P23. P-Shape Volume Profile (Bullish Breakout / Short-Covering Day)
- **Identifying conditions:** The session's Volume Profile chart forms a P-shape — heavy volume built at the lower portion of the day's range (the round part of the P) with a thin upward tail above it. Caused by: initial short sellers getting caught and covering (buy-to-close, aggressive) as price breaks out of a range, combined with new longs entering.
- **What it means:** Bullish trend initiation day with institutional continuation bias. Short sellers' stop-loss buy orders are the fuel. A pullback to the top of the heavy-volume area (VAH of the P's body) becomes the continuation buy level.
- **Confused with:** A random bullish day with no profile structure — distinguished by the distinctly lopsided volume distribution (heavy at bottom/middle, thin at top).
- **Layer:** Layer C / structure.
- **Source:** Wyckoff 2.0 (nuance section: "P-shape bullish trending breakout"; "b-shape bearish trending breakout").

### P24. B-Shape Volume Profile (Bearish Breakout / Long-Liquidation Day)
- **Identifying conditions:** The session's Volume Profile forms a b-shape (inverted P) — heavy volume at the upper portion, thin tail below. Caused by: longs liquidating (sell-to-close, aggressive) as price breaks below a range, combined with new shorts entering.
- **What it means:** Bearish trend initiation day with institutional continuation bias. A rally back to the bottom of the heavy-volume area (VAL of the b's body) becomes the continuation short level.
- **Confused with:** A random bearish day — distinguished by the distinctly lopsided volume distribution (heavy at top, thin at bottom).
- **Layer:** Layer C / structure.
- **Source:** Wyckoff 2.0.

### P25. Finished Auction Pattern (Single-Print Volume Node at Wick Extreme)
- **Identifying conditions:** A very thin, single-print (or near-single-print) LVN appearing at the absolute high or low wick of a session candle — the market briefly visited this level, found no acceptance (almost no volume transacted), and immediately reversed.
- **What it means:** The auction was "finished" at that price — the market collectively rejected it. A finished auction at a session low is a scalp-long trigger targeting rotation back to the session VWAP. A finished auction at a session high is a scalp-short trigger targeting the same.
- **Confused with:** A breakout pullback (which would show more volume at the extreme level) — distinguished by the single/near-zero volume print at the wick extreme; the thinner the volume, the more decisive the rejection.
- **Layer:** Layer C.
- **Source:** Wyckoff 2.0 — "For the Scalper: The Failed Auction" subsection.

### P26. BUEC / LPS (Back-Up to the Edge of the Creek / Last Point of Support)
- **Identifying conditions:** After a Wyckoff SOS (Sign of Strength — the first big breakout bar from a range), price pulls back in a low-volume, narrow-range correction back toward the broken resistance level (now support). This pullback is the BUEC or LPS — the final retest before the Phase E trend launch.
- **Specific identification criteria:**
  - Corrective pullback waves show DECREASING volume vs. the breakout impulse wave (Weis Wave check — see P32).
  - Price does not re-enter the prior range (does not violate the broken VAH/structural level).
  - Order Flow at the BUEC level shows absorption/initiative signals (P9).
- **What it means:** The institutional buyers from the initial breakout are defending their position; the pullback is their accumulation of more at better prices. Entry here with stop behind the VPOC of the new range captures Phase E.
- **Confused with:** A failed breakout (where price re-enters the range) — distinguished by the volume profile of the pullback (low volume, narrow range = BUEC; expanding volume, widening range = failed break).
- **Layer:** Layer C / structure.
- **Source:** Wyckoff 2.0 — "Strategy 3: Continuation Principle" (BUEC entry mechanics); AXIS prior conversation (Wyckoff 2.0 integration).

### P27. Short Squeeze Mechanics (Stop-Loss → Market Order → Accelerant)
- **Identifying conditions:** Price pushes above a well-known resistance level or equal-highs cluster where short sellers have placed stop-loss orders. Stop-losses on short positions are BUY STOP orders → when triggered, they become BUY MARKET orders → print on the ASK column → increase positive Delta → push price higher → trigger more stop-losses above in a chain.
- **What it means:** The mechanical explanation for why a liquidity sweep (P4) can carry far further than "just enough to clear stops" on some days — if the stop-loss density is high enough, the cascade of forced-covering becomes self-reinforcing. This is the institutional use of the exit-mapping rule (F40).
- **Confused with:** Genuine bullish momentum — distinguished by the fact that the move begins immediately above a known stop-cluster zone and often reverses sharply once the stop-buying fuel is exhausted (no genuine new buying motivation; the move was purely mechanical).
- **Layer:** Layer C.
- **Source:** Wyckoff 2.0 — "Order Flow: The Micro-Mechanics" (Position Exit Relations — The Trap subsection, last paragraph on Short Squeeze/Wyckoff Spring mechanics).

### P28. Value Area Re-Entry High-Probability Rotation Rule
- **Identifying conditions:** Price breaks out above VAH (or below VAL), appears to be confirming a breakout, then is rejected and closes a wide-range candle BACK inside the 68.2% Value Area. This rejected re-entry back inside the VA is the confirmation signal.
- **What it means:** High probability (not 100% — the material does not quantify the probability) that price will rotate all the way to the OPPOSITE extreme of the Value Area. If rejected back below VAH → target is VAL. If rejected back above VAL → target is VAH.
- **Confused with:** A simple pullback that will bounce and re-test the breakout level — distinguished by the CLOSING location of the confirmation candle (must close inside the VA, not just touch and bounce back out).
- **Layer:** Layer C / structure.
- **Source:** Wyckoff 2.0 — "Strategy 2: The Reversion Principle" and the Volume Profile section: "If the price tries to break out of a value area but is rejected, it has a very high probability of rotating all the way to the opposite extreme."

### P29. HVN Gravity Well (High Volume Node as Price Magnet)
- **Identifying conditions:** A High Volume Node (HVN) is a price level with a large horizontal peak on the Volume Profile — where the most trading has historically occurred. Price frequently "gravitates" back to HVNs even after excursions away from them, because the fairest price for both buyers and sellers was near the HVN.
- **What it means (two applications):**
  1. **Selling premium**: Writing Iron Condors or Strangles with short strikes at well-established HVNs is effective because price struggles to escape the gravitational pull of the HVN before expiration — high probability of staying within the range.
  2. **Take-profit targeting**: When in a directional trend trade, the next major HVN in the direction of the trend is the primary take-profit target.
- **Confused with:** Support/resistance based purely on prior price highs/lows — HVN is volume-based, not price-extreme-based; a price may never have been an extreme but can still be an HVN.
- **Layer:** Layer C / structure.
- **Source:** Wyckoff 2.0 — "For the Options Trader: Writing Premium at HVNs" subsection.

### P30. LVN Rapid Rejection / V-Turn Zone
- **Identifying conditions:** A Low Volume Node (LVN) is a price level with almost no historical traded volume — a valley in the Volume Profile where neither buyers nor sellers wanted to transact. When price travels into an LVN, it finds no support/resistance and typically moves through it violently (V-turn or rapid continuation), as the "unfair" price attracts no counter-party willing to stand there.
- **What it means:** An LVN is the ideal entry zone for a directional long option buy — the rapid directional movement causes both a Delta gain AND a spike in implied volatility (Vega gain), inflating the option premium multiplicatively. This is the Wyckoff Spring location (the shakeout targets the LVN precisely to clear stops there).
- **Confused with:** A weak support level that will simply be broken — the key is that the directional move through an LVN is fast and often violent (not slow/grinding), making it ideal for options that need a quick move to overcome Theta.
- **Layer:** Layer C / structure.
- **Source:** Wyckoff 2.0 — "For the Options Trader: Buying Directional Volatility at LVNs" subsection.

### P31. Four-Directional Trading Framework (Passarelli's Core Mindset Shift)
- **Identifying conditions:** Not a market pattern — a categorization framework. Every market state maps to one of four tradeable directions:
  1. **UP**: Rising price. Strategy: Buy calls, debit call spreads, sell put spreads.
  2. **DOWN**: Falling price. Strategy: Buy puts, debit put spreads, sell call spreads.
  3. **SIDEWAYS**: Range-bound, low volatility. Strategy: Sell straddles, iron condors, calendar spreads, covered calls. Collect Theta.
  4. **VOLATILE**: Large move expected but direction unknown. Strategy: Buy straddles/strangles, buy options around events. Collect Gamma/Vega.
- **What it means:** Eliminates binary "bullish/bearish" thinking. A "sideways" or "volatile" market read is NOT a non-trade — it's a different strategy selection. The regime (GEX + VIX) determines which of the four directions the market is in, which then drives strategy selection.
- **Source:** *Trading Option Greeks* (Passarelli), explicitly re-stated as one of the four core principles in nifty-options-pro SKILL.md ("Four-directional thinking").

### P32. Weis Wave Exhaustion in Pullbacks
- **Identifying conditions:** After a breakout impulse, a corrective pullback begins. The Weis Wave Indicator shows the volume on each successive corrective wave. A valid BUEC/continuation setup requires that the corrective wave volumes are DECREASING and small relative to the breakout impulse wave volumes.
  - Decreasing, small corrective volume = the counter-trend move has no institutional sponsorship; the breakout will continue.
  - Increasing corrective wave volume = counter-trend has institutional backing; the breakout may fail.
- **What it means:** Volume is the engine of all moves; a low-volume correction in an uptrend proves sellers are not interested in defending lower levels. Used specifically as a filter before entering the BUEC trade (P26).
- **Confused with:** Any pullback being buyable — distinguished by the Weis Wave volume reading on the corrective swings (must show shrinking volume, not expanding).
- **Layer:** Layer C.
- **Source:** Wyckoff 2.0 — "Strategy 3: Continuation Principle" (step 3: "Monitor the corrective waves using the Weis Wave Indicator").

### P33. Jade Lizard (Zero Upside Risk Structure)
- **Identifying conditions:** A three-leg structure combining: Short Put (OTM) + Short Call Spread (Bear Call Spread — sell a closer OTM call, buy a further OTM call). Specifically constructed so that the TOTAL premium received from all three legs EXCEEDS the width of the call spread.
  - If total premium ≥ call spread width: no risk on the upside (the short call spread loss is fully covered by the premium received from the put).
  - All risk is on the downside (below the short put strike minus total premium received).
- **When to deploy:** Slightly bullish to neutral market view; want more premium than a simple credit spread; willing to accept potential assignment/loss on the put side; GEX positive environment (range-bound or mild-upward bias).
- **Confused with:** Iron Condor — distinguished by the fact that the Jade Lizard has NO defined upside risk (vs. Iron Condor's defined loss on both sides) but has undefined downside risk on the put leg. Higher premium but asymmetric risk.
- **Layer:** N/A (strategy structure, not a market pattern).
- **Source:** "Nifty Jade Lizard Strategy" article (NiftyTradingPro); Jade Lizard vs. Iron Condor comparison table.

### P34. Lognormal Distribution Implication (OTM Calls vs. OTM Puts)
- **Identifying conditions:** Not a live-data pattern — a structural pricing fact. Because option pricing models assume stock/index prices follow a lognormal distribution (bounded at zero, unbounded upside), equidistant OTM calls are theoretically worth slightly more than equidistant OTM puts.
- **What it means:** If you see OTM calls and equidistant OTM puts priced the same, the calls are technically underpriced relative to the lognormal assumption. In practice, however, the volatility skew (P17 in main KB) REVERSES this — institutional demand for downside protection bids OTM puts ABOVE their lognormal-fair value, overriding the theoretical lognormal advantage for calls. The result: OTM puts typically trade RICHER than OTM calls (skew effect dominates the lognormal effect for index options like NIFTY).
- **Layer:** N/A (pricing theory layer).
- **Source:** Natenberg — "Phase 1: The Mathematics of Price Distribution" (lognormal implications).

### P35. Conservative Options Overlay (Covered Call / Income Generation Pattern)
- **Identifying conditions:** An existing equity holding (or long futures position) is used as the underlying for option writing — the options position does not introduce new directional risk but generates premium income while dampening downside.
  - **Covered call on stock/ETF**: Own underlying + sell OTM call. Income = premium received. Cap on upside if called away. No additional downside risk beyond the underlying already owned.
  - **Covered call on futures**: Long futures + sell OTM call. Same structure, no cash outlay for the underlying (margin-based).
  - **Collar**: Own underlying + buy OTM put + sell OTM call. Defined range — downside protected, upside capped, net cost often near zero (premium from call sale offsets put purchase cost).
- **What it means:** The "conservative" options use case — using options to reduce net cost basis and generate income, not to speculate. Requires: owning the underlying with conviction, accepting the call-away risk, being prepared to buy more if underlying declines.
- **Layer:** N/A (strategy overlay pattern).
- **Source:** *Options Trading for the Institutional Investor* (Thomsett) — Covered Call section; Kaushik's money-tree system; NSE Bank Nifty booklet (Covered Call / Futures and Collar strategy entries).

### P36. Iceberg Order Pattern (Hidden Passive Institutional Wall)
- **Identifying conditions:** Price repeatedly approaches a level, aggressive market orders hit that level in volume, but price DOES NOT advance through it — the level holds repeatedly despite visible aggressive pressure. On a footprint chart, the ASK column at that price shows consistently large prints (aggressive buyers) but the price refuses to budge.
- **What it means:** A large institutional passive Limit sell order (Iceberg — the visible portion gets filled, then replenishes automatically) is absorbing all aggressive buying. The institution is accumulating a short position or distributing a long position in size, hidden behind the constant replenishment. This is the purest footprint signature of institutional absorption (P9 step 2).
- **Confused with:** Strong resistance at a known level — distinguished by the PERSISTENCE of the stall despite repeat aggressive attempts (a normal resistance level might stop 1–2 attempts; an Iceberg can absorb 10+ waves of buying without budging).
- **Layer:** Layer C.
- **Source:** Wyckoff 2.0 — Delta Divergence Trap section; Institutional algo-architecture ("Iceberg Algorithms" — the same mechanic from both the trading and institutional operations perspectives).

### P37. Market Regime Classification (GEX × VIX → Four Regimes)
- **Identifying conditions:** Four distinct market regimes defined by the intersection of GEX sign and VIX state:
  1. **GEX Positive + VIX Low/Stable**: Range-bound. Expected: Theta strategies (straddle sell, iron condor). Characteristic: dealers dampening moves, Max Pain valid, PCR readable normally.
  2. **GEX Positive + VIX High/Elevated**: Volatile but dampened. Expected: calendar spreads, wait for clarity. Characteristic: IV expensive but dealers still stabilizing — selling premium is risky because of high Vega cost.
  3. **GEX Negative + VIX Low/Weak Low**: Explosive breakout risk imminent. Expected: buy OTM puts/calls, cascade trade setup. Characteristic: complacency + dealer destabilization = maximum cascade risk.
  4. **GEX Negative + VIX High/Rising**: Directional cascade in progress. Expected: follow GEX direction, buy ATM options. Characteristic: dealers amplifying, Vol expanding — the move is real and continuing.
  - Transition/Flip zone (GEX near zero): Reduce size, wait for regime confirmation before trading.
- **What it means:** Strategy selection is entirely regime-determined. The four-directional framework (P31) maps to these four regimes: UP/DOWN covered by Regime 4, SIDEWAYS by Regime 1, VOLATILE by Regime 3, with Regime 2 as the ambiguous "wait" state.
- **Layer:** Meta-layer — combines Layer A (GEX, VIX) to produce the regime that governs all other signal interpretation.
- **Source:** nifty-options-pro SKILL.md — "Quick-Reference: Market Context → Strategy Matrix" (the full table that was described but not formalized in the original KB pass).

### F43. Straddle Breakeven Calculation
- **Inputs/Calculation:**
  - `Upper_Breakeven = ATM_Strike + Total_Premium_Collected`
  - `Lower_Breakeven = ATM_Strike − Total_Premium_Collected`
  - Profit zone at expiry: `Lower_BE < Nifty < Upper_BE`
- **Worked example:** Sell 24,200 CE @₹180 + Sell 24,200 PE @₹160 → Total credit = ₹340 → Upper BE = 24,200 + 340 = 24,540; Lower BE = 24,200 − 340 = 23,860. Profit zone: Nifty closes between 23,860 and 24,540.
- **Cannot tell alone:** Does not account for actual probability that Nifty stays within this range — pair with GEX regime and VIX expected move (F9) to check whether the breakeven range is wide enough relative to the expected daily/weekly move.
- **Source:** nifty-options-pro `strategies_and_setups.md` (Strategy 1 example).

### F44. Iron Condor Max Profit / Max Loss / Breakeven
- **Inputs/Calculation:**
  - Net credit per lot = call spread credit + put spread credit (typical ₹80–120/lot)
  - `Max_Profit = Net_Credit × Lot_Size`
  - `Max_Loss = (Spread_Width − Net_Credit) × Lot_Size` per tested side
  - `Upper_BE = Short_Call_Strike + Net_Credit`
  - `Lower_BE = Short_Put_Strike − Net_Credit`
- **Worked example (Nifty at 24,200, 200-pt wide spreads):** Sell 24,400 CE + Buy 24,600 CE + Sell 24,000 PE + Buy 23,800 PE. Net credit ≈₹100/lot → Max Profit = ₹100 × 25 = ₹2,500. Max Loss = (200 − 100) × 25 = ₹2,500 per tested side.
- **Exit rule built in:** Close at 70–80% of max profit; do NOT wait for last ₹. Adjustment trigger: if Nifty tests either short strike OR P&L loss = 2× credit received → roll or close tested spread.
- **Source:** nifty-options-pro `strategies_and_setups.md` (Strategy 2).

### F45. Vega Gain During IV Expansion (Cascade Leg)
- **Inputs/Calculation:** `Vega_Gain_₹ = Option_Vega × (ΔIV_percentage_points)`
  - ΔIV = new VIX% − old VIX% (in percentage points, not percent change)
- **Worked example (June 23 2026):** VIX moved from 12.89 to 14.05 = +1.16 percentage points. An option with Vega = 1.0 gained ₹1.16 from vol expansion ALONE (on top of whatever Delta gain came from the price move).
- **Cannot tell alone:** This is the Vega component only — must be added to the Delta component (F46) to get the total option gain. The multiplicative/additive combination of both is why puts gained 276–425% on June 23 (both components fired simultaneously).
- **Source:** AXIS prior conversation — trap-mechanism document (June 22–23 example, Vega gain calculation).

### F46. Total Option Gain in a Cascade (Additive Model)
- **Inputs/Calculation:** `Total_Option_Gain = Delta_Gain + Vega_Gain`
  - `Delta_Gain = Delta × ΔSpot_Points`
  - `Vega_Gain = Vega × ΔIV_percentage_points` (F45)
  - As a % of initial premium: `Gain% = Total_Gain / Initial_Premium × 100`
- **Worked example (June 23 2026 — 23,850 PE @ ₹12):** Spot moved ~250 pts down; Delta ≈0.15 (OTM put) → Delta_Gain ≈ 0.15 × 250 = ₹37.50. VIX +1.16 pts; Vega ≈1.0 → Vega_Gain ≈₹1.16. But Delta also increases as put goes further ITM (Gamma effect — Delta grows from 0.15 to ~0.50 as spot falls 250 pts) → actual gain was ₹12 → ₹30+ (~150%+). The 276–425% gains mentioned in the source were at earlier strikes (closer to ATM) that started with higher Delta and Vega.
- **Why this matters for AXIS:** When GEX is negative + VIX is at a Weak Low, BOTH Delta gain and Vega gain fire in the same direction simultaneously — this is the mathematical reason why cascade trades can produce multibagger option returns even from OTM entries.
- **Source:** AXIS prior conversation; nifty-options-pro `market_structure_gex.md` (June 23 example).

### F47. Vertical Debit Spread — Max Profit / Loss / Breakeven
- **Inputs/Calculation (Bullish Debit Call Spread):**
  - Buy lower-strike CE (ATM) + Sell higher-strike CE (OTM)
  - `Net_Debit = Buy_Premium − Sell_Premium`
  - `Max_Profit = (Spread_Width − Net_Debit) × Lot_Size`
  - `Max_Loss = Net_Debit × Lot_Size`
  - `Breakeven = Lower_Strike + Net_Debit`
- **Worked example (Nifty at 24,200, bullish):** Buy 24,200 CE @₹180 + Sell 24,400 CE @₹80. Net debit = ₹100 × 25 = ₹2,500. Max profit = (200 − 100) × 25 = ₹2,500. Breakeven = 24,200 + 100 = 24,300. Max profit achieved at or above 24,400 expiry.
- **Advantage over naked long:** Lower breakeven, lower Vega exposure, significantly lower Theta cost — the sold OTM option hedges the Theta drain of the bought ATM option.
- **Source:** nifty-options-pro `strategies_and_setups.md` (Strategy 4: Vertical Debit Spread).

### F48. Long Straddle/Strangle — Breakeven and Conditions
- **Inputs/Calculation:**
  - Straddle: Buy ATM CE + Buy ATM PE (same strike, same expiry). Requires LARGE move in either direction.
  - `Upper_BE = Strike + Total_Premium_Paid`
  - `Lower_BE = Strike − Total_Premium_Paid`
  - `Max_Loss = Total_Premium_Paid × Lot_Size` (if Nifty closes exactly at strike at expiry)
  - Strangle: Buy OTM CE + Buy OTM PE (different strikes, cheaper, needs bigger move). Same breakeven formula applies to each leg's strike separately.
- **When to deploy (4 specific conditions from source):**
  1. 2–3 days before a major event (RBI, elections, Budget) when IV is not yet fully inflated.
  2. When VIX is at a historical/structural low (cheap options, asymmetric risk upside).
  3. When GEX has JUST flipped negative but direction not yet confirmed.
  4. When the VIX expected move (F9) is significantly larger than the straddle cost (positive expected value for the buy).
- **Time stop:** If the large move doesn't happen within the expected timeframe — exit regardless of P&L.
- **Source:** nifty-options-pro `strategies_and_setups.md` (Strategy 5: Long Straddle/Strangle).

### F49. Calendar Spread — Mechanics and Profit Logic
- **Inputs/Calculation:**
  - Sell near-expiry ATM option (short Theta, faster decay)
  - Buy far-expiry ATM option (long Vega protection, slower decay)
  - Net cost = Far_Premium − Near_Premium
  - Profit source: near-dated option decays faster than far-dated → spread widens in value as time passes.
  - Risk: a large sudden move in either direction makes BOTH options lose value simultaneously (long Vega on far leg gets hurt if IV collapses; short Gamma on near leg gets hurt if price moves away from strike).
- **Best conditions for use:**
  - Low IV environment (cheap far-dated protection).
  - Normal term structure (front-month IV ≤ back-month IV).
  - Stable/sideways price expected near term.
  - Close before front-month expiry once near-dated premium has largely decayed.
- **Source:** nifty-options-pro `strategies_and_setups.md` (Strategy 7: Calendar Spread).

### F50. Jade Lizard — Construction Formula and Zero Upside Risk Condition
- **Inputs/Calculation:**
  - Leg 1: Sell OTM Put (e.g., 22,800 PE @₹50)
  - Leg 2: Sell OTM Call (e.g., 23,300 CE @₹45)
  - Leg 3: Buy further OTM Call (e.g., 23,400 CE @₹25)
  - `Total_Premium = Put_Premium + Short_Call_Premium − Long_Call_Premium = 50 + 45 − 25 = ₹70/unit = ₹1,750/lot`
  - `Zero Upside Risk Condition: Total_Premium ≥ Call_Spread_Width` (70 ≥ 100 in this example — DOES NOT meet it; a valid Jade Lizard needs Total_Premium > Spread_Width = 100. In the source example the setup is illustrative — actual values must be checked.)
  - `Downside risk starts below: Put_Strike − Total_Premium = 22,800 − 70 = 22,730`
  - `Upside risk: zero if condition met; normal call spread max loss otherwise`
- **Jade Lizard vs Iron Condor comparison (from source):**
  | Feature | Jade Lizard | Iron Condor |
  |---|---|---|
  | Upside risk | Zero (if structured) | Defined loss |
  | Downside risk | Undefined (naked put) | Defined loss |
  | Premium | Higher | Lower |
  | Best for | Bullish bias | Neutral |
- **Source:** "Nifty Jade Lizard Strategy" article (NiftyTradingPro, Apr 2026).

### F51. F&O Turnover Calculation (Indian Tax)
- **Inputs/Calculation:** `F&O_Turnover = Absolute_Sum_of_All_Profits + Absolute_Sum_of_All_Losses` (NOT net P&L — the absolute values are added regardless of sign)
- **Example:** +₹50,000 winning trades + ₹30,000 losing trades = ₹80,000 F&O turnover (even though net P&L is only +₹20,000).
- **Tax audit thresholds:**
  - Turnover > ₹10 crore → mandatory tax audit under Section 44AB.
  - Turnover > ₹1 crore AND profit < 6% of turnover → presumptive audit may apply under Section 44AD.
- **Income classification:** Non-speculative business income under Section 43(5), Income Tax Act.
- **Loss carry-forward:** F&O losses can be carried forward 8 years; set off against future business income only (NOT against salary income).
- **Deductible expenses:** STT paid, brokerage, exchange charges, internet, software subscriptions (all deductible against F&O income).
- **Separate bank account:** Recommended — maintain a dedicated account for all F&O trading transactions.
- **Source:** nifty-options-pro `risk_and_adjustments.md` (Tax Implications); NiftyTradingPro Earnings Season article.

### F53. Gamma Scalping Trigger Points and Execution
- **Inputs/Calculation:**
  - Entry condition: Long an ATM straddle (or long Gamma position).
  - Trigger: Nifty moves 50–75 points in one direction from entry.
  - Delta drift check: Position Delta has moved from ~0 to ±30–40 (i.e., position has become directionally biased).
  - `Scalp Action (Long Straddle, Nifty rises 75 pts): Sell Nifty Futures = Drifted_Delta × Lots × 25` to bring net Delta back to 0.
  - `Scalp Action (Nifty falls 75 pts): Buy Nifty Futures` to bring Delta back to 0.
  - Repeat every 50–75 pt move throughout the session.
- **When this works:** High Gamma (near expiry, ATM options), large intraday swings with reversals. Best on expiry day with negative GEX (maximum Gamma, maximum OI, maximum cascade volatility).
- **When this fails:** A trending day with no reversals — you repeatedly hedge against the trend, each time cutting a profit by buying/selling against the direction.
- **P&L logic:** Each scalp locks in a small profit from the swing. Sum of all scalp profits must exceed total Theta decay on the straddle for the day to be net profitable. If the underlying volatility (realized vol) exceeds the IV implied by the straddle cost, the strategy wins.
- **Source:** nifty-options-pro `risk_and_adjustments.md` (Gamma Scalping); *Trading Option Greeks* (Passarelli, Part III).

### F64. Volume Profile Shape Identification Rules
- **P-shape (bullish breakout):**
  - Heavy volume in the LOWER portion of the day's range.
  - Thin upward tail above.
  - Price closes NEAR THE HIGH of the day.
  - Reading: Short sellers being squeezed + new longs entering → institutional buying confirmation.
  - Trading rule: A pullback to the top of the heavy-volume area (the VAH of the P's body) = next-session continuation buy entry.
- **b-shape (bearish breakout):**
  - Heavy volume in the UPPER portion of the day's range.
  - Thin downward tail below.
  - Price closes NEAR THE LOW of the day.
  - Reading: Long holders liquidating + new shorts entering → institutional selling confirmation.
  - Trading rule: A rally back to the bottom of the heavy-volume area (the VAL of the b's body) = next-session continuation short entry.
- **D-shape (normal balanced day):**
  - Volume distributed normally across the range; bell-curve-like profile.
  - Reading: No strong directional bias; both sides active.
  - Trading rule: Range trade strategy appropriate; sell premium at VAH/VAL edges.
- **Source:** Wyckoff 2.0 — "Strategy 3: Continuation Principle" (P-shape/b-shape identification); "Strategy 1: Trading Range Principle" (D-shape/balanced).

### F65. ANOVA Decomposition (Variance Explained by Model)
- **Inputs/Calculation:** `Var(Yi) = Var(E[Yi|Xi]) + E[Var(Yi|Xi)]`
  - Left side: Total variance in the outcome (e.g., total variance in NIFTY next-session return).
  - First term (Explained): Variance explained by the model/CEF (e.g., how much the Direction Scorer explains).
  - Second term (Unexplained): Residual variance the model cannot capture (noise).
  - R² = Explained/Total = Var(E[Y|X]) / Var(Y).
- **Tells you alone:** How much of the outcome's variation the model accounts for. An R² of 0.10 (10%) on daily returns is actually considered decent for financial prediction — this framework helps you not over-expect from the Direction Scorer.
- **Source:** QUANT BIBLE (Econometrics section).

### F66. Backtesting Quality Metrics (Institutional Standard)
- **Sharpe Ratio:** `SR = (Strategy_Return − Risk_Free_Rate) / Volatility_of_Strategy_Returns`. Higher is better; >1.0 considered acceptable, >2.0 considered strong for systematic strategies.
- **Sortino Ratio:** `Sortino = (Strategy_Return − Risk_Free_Rate) / Downside_Deviation` (only measures volatility of negative returns — penalizes downside risk, not upside). More appropriate for options strategies where the return distribution is asymmetric.
- **Maximum Drawdown:** `Max_DD = (Peak_Value − Trough_Value) / Peak_Value`. The worst peak-to-trough loss experienced. Critical constraint: must remain within tolerable limits (typically ≤ 20–30% for a systematic strategy) regardless of annual return.
- **Win Rate vs. Expectancy trade-off:** Institutional algos often win only 51–55% of the time — profitability comes from expectancy (F26 in main KB) and position sizing (Kelly Criterion, F25), NOT from win rate.
- **Source:** Wyckoff 2.0 — Institutional Quantitative Blueprint section (Sharpe, Sortino, Max Drawdown as backtesting evaluation metrics).

### F67. Market Making Core Determinants (3-Factor Model)
- **Inputs/Calculation:** Every market maker's bid and ask are set by exactly three factors simultaneously:
  1. **Theoretical Value (TV):** What the mathematical model calculates as the true worth of the asset. The midpoint of the bid-ask spread starts here. Wider spread when uncertain (unknown underlying, obscure metric); tighter spread when certain (standard die roll — exact EV = 3.5).
  2. **Last Traded Price (LTP):** What the market most recently transacted. Acts as an anchor — if LTP has moved away from TV, the market maker must decide whether to trust their model or update it.
  3. **Current Position (Inventory):** If the market maker is long too much of the asset, they SKEW their bid-ask lower (give up edge on the ask to incentivize buyers to take the asset off their hands). If short too much, skew higher. The skew is the sacrifice of theoretical profit margin to reduce inventory risk.
- **Informational asymmetry rule:** If a counterparty repeatedly buys from you as you raise your price, they likely believe the true value is higher than your TV. Anchor your entire market upward — they'll still buy, now at better prices for you.
- **Trader Memory rule:** Must track the exact prices at which each counterparty has previously bought or sold to build an internal model of what they believe the value is.
- **Source:** QUANT BIBLE (Market Making Theory section).

### F68. Value Area Probability Rules (VA Statistics)
- **Inputs/Calculation:** From the Gaussian bell-curve application to Volume Profile:
  - 1 SD (the Value Area, 68.2%): the price range where the "majority" of participants agreed.
  - 2 SD (95.4%): extreme boundaries; price rarely spends time here.
  - 3 SD (99.7%): statistical anomaly territory; not a normal price event.
  - Rejection Area (outside VA, 31.8%): "unfair" prices that the market quickly corrected.
- **Key operational rules derived:**
  - If price is INSIDE the VA: Market is in equilibrium — expect continuation/chop; range-trade strategies preferred.
  - If price breaks OUTSIDE the VA with VOLUME: Market is in imbalance — trend strategies preferred; expect either rapid VA return (failed breakout) or acceleration toward new VA (genuine breakout).
  - The direction of a failed-breakout rotation is ALWAYS toward the OPPOSITE VA extreme (P28 in this supplement).
- **Source:** Wyckoff 2.0 — "Volume Profile: The Objective Valuation" section.

### IR35. Market Making Inventory Skew Rule
- **IF** acting as a market maker (or managing a short-premium book) and net inventory has drifted long (too much of an asset held)
- **THEN** skew the bid-ask spread lower — lower your ask price (give up some theoretical edge) to incentivize counterparties to BUY from you and reduce your long inventory. The goal is NOT to maximize per-trade profit; it is to manage inventory risk first.
- **IF** net inventory has drifted short (too many assets sold/short)
- **THEN** skew the bid-ask spread higher — raise your bid (pay slightly more than theoretical to incentivize sellers to come to you).
- **CONFIDENCE: HIGH** — core market-making principle stated in QUANT BIBLE and institutional algo commentary. Directly applicable to AXIS's short-premium management: when the book is directionally imbalanced (delta has drifted significantly in one direction from the straddle/condor), the correct response is the mechanical hedge (IR23, Greek Drift Monitoring) rather than hoping for reversion.
- **Source:** QUANT BIBLE (Market Making — Strategy B: Skewing for Inventory).

### IR36. Market Making Width Rule (Uncertainty → Wider Spread)
- **IF** quoting a market on an asset with HIGH uncertainty (unknown true value, exotic metric, ambiguous data)
- **THEN** widen the bid-ask spread proportionally — never quote so wide that no trades occur (defeats the purpose) but wide enough to protect against an informed counterparty exploiting your uncertainty.
- **IF** quoting a market on an asset with LOW uncertainty (standard, well-modeled, well-understood)
- **THEN** keep the spread very tight (2–3x minimum tick) — the tightest, fastest markets get the majority of trades.
- **Application to AXIS:** When the Direction Scorer output is ambiguous (score = 3, neither bullish nor bearish), the correct response is NOT to force a trade — it is to "widen the spread" (i.e., stand aside or reduce to minimum size) until the regime becomes clear. Trading in an ambiguous regime is equivalent to quoting a market when you don't know the TV.
- **CONFIDENCE: HIGH** for the principle; QUANT BIBLE states "The tightest and fastest markets usually get the majority of trades" and "Widen the spread based on uncertainty."
- **Source:** QUANT BIBLE (Market Making — Strategy A: Setting the Initial Market).

### IR37. Informational Asymmetry Update Rule

### IR55. Covered Call Ratio Balancing Rule (Kaushik/Thomsett)
- **IF** writing covered calls against an ETF or stock portfolio
- **THEN** the number of call contracts written must EXACTLY equal the number of underlying units held divided by the lot size. Writing MORE contracts than you can cover creates a "naked" short call on the excess — undefined risk.
  - `Max_Covered_Calls = Floor(Units_Held / Lot_Size)`
  - Example: 7,500 NIFTY BeES units / 25 per lot = 300 lots maximum covered.
  - If some units are called away and portfolio falls below 7,500 units → stop writing calls until units are restored to ≥7,500.
- **CONFIDENCE: HIGH** — explicitly stated in both Thomsett ("balance the number of call options sold with the equivalent number of shares held") and Kaushik (7,500 minimum unit threshold to begin writing).
- **Source:** *Options Trading Handbook* (Kaushik); *Options Trading for the Institutional Investor* (Thomsett).

### P39. Gamma-Theta Perpetual Opposition (Structural Position Constraint)
- **Identifying conditions:** Not a market pattern — a mathematical constraint on every options position:
  - If you are LONG options (any type): you have positive Gamma AND negative Theta simultaneously, always, without exception.
  - If you are SHORT options (any type): you have negative Gamma AND positive Theta simultaneously, always, without exception.
  - There is NO options position that simultaneously has positive Gamma (accelerating profits on moves) AND positive Theta (earning time decay) — this combination is mathematically impossible for vanilla options.
- **What it means:** Every options trade forces you to choose which side of this tradeoff to be on. This is the fundamental tension of options trading, and every strategy exists somewhere on this spectrum:
  - Pure Theta-collector (short straddle) = maximum positive Theta + maximum negative Gamma
  - Pure Gamma-buyer (long straddle) = maximum positive Gamma + maximum negative Theta
  - Spreads = partial offset of both (limited Gamma + limited Theta)
- **Confused with:** The idea that you can construct a "safe" position that collects Theta without significant Gamma risk — the tradeoff is always present; spreads only reduce it, they don't eliminate it.
- **Layer:** N/A (fundamental pricing constraint).
- **Source:** *Trading Option Greeks* (Passarelli) — explicitly stated: "Long Gamma = paying Theta. Short Gamma = collecting Theta but exposed to explosive moves"; nifty-options-pro SKILL.md (same language).

### P40. IV vs. HV Divergence (Volatility Mispricing Signal)
- **Identifying conditions:**
  - **IV significantly exceeds HV (IV >> HV):** Options are priced expensively relative to what the underlying has historically moved. Sellers have the mathematical edge.
  - **IV approximately equals HV (IV ≈ HV):** Options are fairly priced; no structural edge for either buyer or seller.
  - **IV below HV (IV < HV):** Options are priced cheaply relative to actual historical movement. Buyers have the mathematical edge (the underlying has been moving more than the market expects).
- **What it means:** This is Natenberg's casino-edge concept (P21 in main KB) made operational. The gap between IV and HV is the mispricing a professional exploits — sell when IV is rich (IV >> HV), buy when IV is cheap (IV < HV).
- **Confused with:** VIX level alone (high VIX just means high IV — it doesn't tell you if IV is high or low RELATIVE to actual realized movement; you need HV for comparison).
- **Layer:** Layer A (available from any options platform — Sensibull/Opstra show IV; HV can be calculated from historical price data).
- **Source:** nifty-options-pro `greeks_and_pricing.md` ("IV vs HV edge" section); Natenberg.

### P42. Probability of Profit (POP) vs. Expected Value (EV) — Distinct Metrics
- **Identifying conditions:** Both metrics assess a strategy's quality, but they measure different things and can give opposite conclusions:
  - **POP (Probability of Profit):** The percentage of trades that will end profitably. An iron condor placed 200+ pts OTM has POP of 55–65% — it will be profitable more than half the time.
  - **EV (Expected Value):** The probability-weighted outcome INCLUDING the magnitude of losses when they occur. A strategy can have 60% POP but negative EV if the 40% of losing trades are large enough to outweigh the 60% of winning trades.
- **Example that demonstrates the gap:** An iron condor with max profit ₹2,500 and max loss ₹2,500 at 60% POP: EV = (0.60 × ₹2,500) − (0.40 × ₹2,500) = ₹1,500 − ₹1,000 = +₹500. But if the same condor has max loss ₹7,500 (wider spread): EV = (0.60 × ₹2,500) − (0.40 × ₹7,500) = ₹1,500 − ₹3,000 = −₹1,500 (NEGATIVE EV despite 60% win rate).
- **What it means for AXIS:** Agent 2 must compute EV (including transaction costs), NOT just POP. A 65% POP strategy with negative EV should be blocked; a 45% POP strategy with positive EV should be passed.
- **Confused with:** Treating high POP as equivalent to good strategy — they are genuinely different metrics.
- **Layer:** N/A (analytical framework).
- **Source:** nifty-options-pro SKILL.md (iron condor "55–65% probability of profit" disclosure — which prompted this gap identification); QUANT BIBLE (Expected Value vs. win rate distinction).

### P43. Short Position Exit → Buy Order → Price Acceleration (Stop-Loss Cascade)
- **Identifying conditions:** When a large number of short-sellers (or put-writers who are short NIFTY futures as delta-hedges) have stop-losses clustered at a level ABOVE current price:
  1. Price rises to that level.
  2. Stop-losses trigger → they become BUY orders (Buy Stop → executed as Buy Market).
  3. These buy orders hit the ASK → print in the ASK column → increase positive Delta.
  4. The additional demand from these forced-buy orders pushes price higher.
  5. Higher price triggers MORE stop-losses above → chain reaction.
- **What it means:** This is the mechanical explanation for WHY price often "runs" much further than fundamentals suggest after triggering a well-known stop cluster. It is also the institutional use case for the liquidity sweep (P4 in main KB) — institutions identify where stop clusters are, push price there deliberately, then reverse once the stop-buying fuel is exhausted.
- **Inverse (for longs going down):** Long positions → stop-loss = Sell Stop → becomes Sell Market → hits BID → drives price lower → triggers more long stops → cascade.
- **Layer:** Layer C (order-flow cascade mechanics).
- **Source:** Wyckoff 2.0 — "Order Flow: The Micro-Mechanics" (Position Exit Relations — closing a Short via stop-loss → Buy Market → ASK); Bookmap/footprint orderflow note.

### P44. IV Rank / IV Percentile (Relative IV Context)
- **Identifying conditions:** Not a market pattern but a missing context measure. Absolute IV (e.g., VIX = 14) is not sufficient to determine if options are cheap or expensive — you need IV RELATIVE to its own historical range.
  - **IV Rank:** `IV_Rank = (Current_IV − 52_week_low_IV) / (52_week_high_IV − 52_week_low_IV) × 100`. If IV Rank = 80, current IV is near the top of its annual range (expensive; prefer selling).
  - **IV Percentile:** Percentage of days in the past year where IV was LOWER than today. If IV Percentile = 80%, IV is higher than 80% of all days this past year.
- **Trading rules by IV Rank:**
  - IV Rank > 50 → options relatively expensive → prefer selling premium (straddle, condor, strangle).
  - IV Rank < 30 → options relatively cheap → prefer buying premium or defined-risk directional spreads.
  - IV Rank 30–50 → neutral zone → calendar spreads or wait.
- **What it means for expiry selection in AXIS Option Chain Selector (Component 5):** If IV Rank is high AND it's within the weekly expiry window, sell the weekly (expensive premium). If IV Rank is low AND event risk exists in the week, buy the next-monthly (cheap protection, avoid IV-crush risk on the current week).

### P45. "Frictionless Market" — The BS Model's Unrealistic Assumptions and Real Corrections
- **The 5 Black-Scholes assumptions (from Natenberg — all of which the model requires but reality violates):**
  1. Underlying price changes are ENTIRELY RANDOM — future direction cannot be predicted.
  2. PERCENTAGE changes in the underlying are normally distributed.
  3. Because changes are continuously compounded → actual PRICES at expiry are lognormally distributed.
  4. The mean of this lognormal distribution sits at the FORWARD PRICE (not spot price).
  5. Trading is CONTINUOUS and FRICTIONLESS (no transaction costs, identical borrow/lend rates, no daily limits).
- **Real-world corrections required for AXIS:**
  - Assumption 5 is violated in Indian markets: NSE has circuit-breaker halts; options have bid-ask spreads; there are STT and brokerage costs (₹85–95/lot per F35 in main KB); borrowing and lending rates differ.

  - Correction for circuit breakers: in extreme cascade events (P6 in main KB), trading may halt temporarily — position cannot be adjusted during this window. Size down when cascade risk is high.
  - Correction for lognormal (vs. normal): deep OTM puts are structurally more expensive than BS predicts due to fat-tail/crash risk demand (the volatility skew, P17 in main KB).

### F69. All Four Vertical Spread Types — Complete Reference
**From Passarelli (Trading Option Greeks, Part II — Spreads):**

**Bull Call Spread (Debit — Bullish)**
- Buy lower-strike Call + Sell higher-strike Call (same expiry)
- `Net_Debit = Buy_Premium − Sell_Premium`
- `Max_Profit = (Spread_Width − Net_Debit) × Lot_Size` (achieved at or above short strike at expiry)
- `Max_Loss = Net_Debit × Lot_Size` (if underlying closes at or below long strike at expiry)
- `Breakeven = Long_Strike + Net_Debit`
- Use: moderately to strongly bullish with defined risk; lowers breakeven vs. naked call buy; reduces Vega and Theta cost.

**Bear Call Spread (Credit — Bearish)**
- Sell lower-strike Call + Buy higher-strike Call (same expiry)
- `Net_Credit = Sell_Premium − Buy_Premium`
- `Max_Profit = Net_Credit × Lot_Size` (if underlying closes at or below short call strike at expiry)
- `Max_Loss = (Spread_Width − Net_Credit) × Lot_Size`
- `Breakeven = Short_Strike + Net_Credit`
- Use: bearish or mildly bearish with premium income; Theta works for you; defined risk vs. naked call sale.

**Bear Put Spread (Debit — Bearish)**
- Buy higher-strike Put + Sell lower-strike Put (same expiry)
- `Net_Debit = Buy_Premium − Sell_Premium`
- `Max_Profit = (Spread_Width − Net_Debit) × Lot_Size` (if underlying closes at or below short put strike)
- `Max_Loss = Net_Debit × Lot_Size`
- `Breakeven = Long_Strike − Net_Debit`
- Use: moderately to strongly bearish with defined risk; the "short put" leg hedges Theta cost; ideal when expecting drop to a specific support level, not a complete crash.

**Bull Put Spread (Credit — Bullish)**
- Sell higher-strike Put + Buy lower-strike Put (same expiry)
- `Net_Credit = Sell_Premium − Buy_Premium`
- `Max_Profit = Net_Credit × Lot_Size` (if underlying closes at or above short put strike at expiry)
- `Max_Loss = (Spread_Width − Net_Credit) × Lot_Size`
- `Breakeven = Short_Strike − Net_Credit`
- Use: bullish or mildly bullish with premium income; Theta works for you; alternative to naked put sale with defined downside.

- **Source:** *Trading Option Greeks* (Passarelli) — Vertical Spreads chapter; NSE Bank Nifty Options Strategies booklet (Call Spread + Put Spread entries).

### F70. Long Call Butterfly — Full Construction and P&L
**From NSE Bank Nifty Options Strategies booklet:**
- **Construction:** Sell 2 ATM Calls + Buy 1 ITM Call + Buy 1 OTM Call (all same expiry, equidistant strikes)
  - Equivalently: 1 Bull Call Spread + 1 Bear Call Spread sharing the same short ATM strike
- **Example (strikes: 22,800 / 23,000 / 23,200, ATM = 23,000):**
  - Buy 22,800 CE + Sell 2× 23,000 CE + Buy 23,200 CE
  - `Net_Debit = (Buy_22800_CE + Buy_23200_CE) − 2×Sell_23000_CE`
  - `Max_Profit = (Adjacent_Strike_Width − Net_Debit) × Lot_Size` (if underlying closes exactly at the short strike at expiry)
  - `Max_Loss = Net_Debit × Lot_Size`
  - `Upper_BE = Short_Strike + Net_Debit`; `Lower_BE = Short_Strike − Net_Debit`
- **Market view:** Neutral direction + bearish on volatility (expects underlying to stay near ATM).
- **Greek profile:** Negative Gamma (benefits from no movement), positive Theta, negative Vega (benefits from falling IV).
- **Volatility skew impact on selection (from Passarelli):** If OTM puts trade at significantly higher IV than equidistant OTM calls (vertical skew), an Iron Butterfly (calls + puts) may be preferable to a pure Call Butterfly because the put side of the Iron Butterfly "sells the skew premium."
- **Source:** NSE Bank Nifty Options Strategies booklet (Neutral Strategies table — Long Call Butterfly entry); *Trading Option Greeks* (Passarelli — Wing Spreads chapter on skew impact).

### F71. Long Call Condor — Construction and P&L
**From NSE Bank Nifty Options Strategies booklet:**
- **Construction:** Buy 1 lower ITM Call + Sell 1 lower-middle ITM Call + Sell 1 higher-middle OTM Call + Buy 1 higher OTM Call (all same expiry, equidistant wing spacing)
- **P&L profile:**
  - `Max_Profit` = achieved when underlying closes between the two short strikes at expiry (the "body" of the condor)
  - `Max_Loss = Net_Debit × Lot_Size` (if underlying closes outside either outer wing at expiry)
  - Profit zone is WIDER than a butterfly (four strikes instead of three → wider profitable range)
  - Net debit is HIGHER than a butterfly (buying two long legs instead of one)
- **Market view:** Range-bound market with slightly wider range expectation than butterfly.
- **Key difference from Iron Condor:** A Long Call Condor uses ONLY calls (a debit spread). An Iron Condor uses both calls and puts (a credit spread). The Iron Condor is far more common in practice because selling premium (credit) is more capital-efficient and avoids the debit cost.
- **Source:** NSE Bank Nifty Options Strategies booklet (Neutral Strategies table — Long Call Condor entry).

### F72. IV Rank Formula (Missing from Main KB and Part 1)
- **Inputs/Calculation:**
  - `IV_Rank = (Current_IV − 52_Week_Low_IV) / (52_Week_High_IV − 52_Week_Low_IV) × 100`
  - `IV_Percentile = (Number_of_Days_in_Past_Year_Where_IV_Was_Lower_Than_Today / 252) × 100`
- **Trading thresholds (from `greeks_and_pricing.md` context):**
  - IV Rank > 50 → options expensive → prefer selling premium
  - IV Rank < 30 → options cheap → prefer buying premium or directional spreads
  - IV Rank 30–50 → neutral / calendar spread zone
- **Cannot tell alone:** IV Rank tells you IF options are expensive relative to history, NOT whether the current level of volatility will continue or reverse. Must combine with GEX regime — high IV Rank in a positive-GEX environment (sell premium) is very different from high IV Rank in a negative-GEX environment (IV may keep expanding; do NOT sell naked premium).
- **Source:** nifty-options-pro SKILL.md (referenced in expiry selection logic, F56 Part 1 Supplement); P44 in this Part 2.

### F73. Delta as Probability Proxy — Formal Strike Selection Rule
- **Inputs/Calculation:**
  - For a European-style option (which NIFTY options are), option Delta approximates the probability of that strike expiring ITM at expiry:
    - ATM Call/Put Delta ≈ 0.50 → ~50% probability of expiring ITM
    - Delta 0.35 → ~35% probability of expiring ITM
    - Delta 0.15 → ~15% probability of expiring ITM
    - Delta 0.05 → ~5% probability of expiring ITM
  - This is an approximation (ignores risk-free rate effects and lognormal distribution), but it is the standard market-maker and retail practitioner convention.
- **Strike selection implications:**
  - Directional buy sweet spot (Delta 0.35–0.55): 35–55% probability of expiring ITM → roughly 50-50 odds at ATM, dropping to 35% for 1 strike OTM. This is the range where leverage is meaningful without overpaying for very-low-probability outcomes.
  - Iron Condor short legs (Delta ~0.15–0.20): 15–20% probability of expiring ITM → 80–85% probability of expiring worthless → this is the "55–65% probability of profit" range for the whole condor (both legs must stay OTM simultaneously).
  - Far OTM buys (Delta < 0.10): <10% probability of expiring ITM → statistically expected to fail 90%+ of the time. AXIS rule: NEVER buy for directional plays; only permissible as tail-risk hedges or specific cascade-signal trades (Strategy 6 in `strategies_and_setups.md`).
- **Source:** nifty-options-pro `greeks_and_pricing.md` ("Delta as probability proxy" section); *Trading Option Greeks* (Passarelli).

### F74. NIFTY Options Instrument Facts (Structural Reference)
- **Option style:** European (can ONLY be exercised at expiry — not before). This means early exercise is NEVER an issue for NIFTY options. There is no assignment risk before expiry for option sellers (unlike American-style options on individual stocks).
- **Settlement:** Cash-settled. No physical delivery of NIFTY units — the P&L is settled in cash based on the final settlement price (NIFTY's closing value on expiry day, calculated as average of last 30 minutes of trading).
- **Lot size:** 25 units (current as of 2023, confirmed 2026). See lot-size inconsistency note in Coverage Check of main KB — some older sources use 75 or 50.
- **Expiry structure:**
  - Weekly expiry: every THURSDAY (near-week contract)
  - Next-weekly: two Thursdays out
  - Monthly expiry: last Thursday of each calendar month
  - Far-monthly: available but low liquidity beyond 3 months
- **Trading hours:** 9:15 AM to 3:30 PM IST (regular session). Pre-open: 9:00–9:15 AM (order entry but no matching).
- **Circuit breakers:** NSE halts F&O trading if NIFTY moves ±10% (15-min halt), ±15% (45-min halt), ±20% (session halt for the day). These override continuous-trading assumptions of Black-Scholes (P45 in this Part 2).
- **SEBI margin framework:** As of 2026 — SPAN + Exposure margin required for option sellers and futures. Option BUYERS only pay premium. Full margin tables in F36 of main KB.
- **Source:** NSE website structure; nifty-options-pro SKILL.md and multiple articles; "Nifty Lot Size and Margin Explained" article.

### F76. Natenberg's "The Race" — Formal Rule Statement
- **Concept:** Every delta-neutral position in a long-options (positive Gamma) strategy involves a race between two opposing forces:
  1. **Theta (The Opponent):** Daily time decay that costs the long-options holder a fixed amount per day (negative Theta means you lose money every day the market doesn't move).
  2. **Gamma-Scalping Cash Flow (The Weapon):** When you continuously rebalance your delta-neutral position, you are mechanically FORCED to buy low and sell high on the underlying — this generates positive cash flow regardless of trend direction.
- **The mathematical outcome:**
  - If you bought the option BELOW its theoretical value (IV < future realized volatility): the cash flow from gamma-scalping OUTPACES the Theta decay → net profit.
  - If you bought the option AT theoretical value: cash flow ≈ Theta decay → breakeven.
  - If you bought the option ABOVE theoretical value (IV > future realized volatility): Theta decay outpaces the cash flow → net loss (this is why selling overpriced options works).
- **AXIS application:** The Direction Scorer + VIX Weak Low detection system is specifically trying to identify moments where IV is structurally cheap (the option is priced below expected future realized volatility), making the Gamma-scalping buyer the winner in this race. Negative GEX + VIX Weak Low = the cascade hasn't happened yet, meaning actual realized volatility will significantly exceed current IV → the "race" is won by the option buyer before it even starts.
- **Source:** Natenberg — "Phase 5: The Ultimate Conclusion (The Race)"; captured in E29 of Supplement Part 1 as an example but not as a formal rule.

### F78. Iron Butterfly — Construction and When to Use Over Iron Condor
- **Construction:** Sell ATM Call + Sell ATM Put (same strike, same expiry) + Buy OTM Call (wing) + Buy OTM Put (wing)
  - This is a SHORT STRADDLE at the ATM strike with LONG wings for protection
  - Max profit = full ATM straddle credit (vs. Iron Condor's partial OTM credit) — HIGHER max profit
  - Profit zone is NARROWER than an Iron Condor (the ATM position requires underlying to stay very close to strike)
  - `Max_Profit = (ATM_Credit − Wing_Cost) × Lot_Size`
  - `Max_Loss = (Wing_Width − Net_Credit) × Lot_Size`
- **Iron Butterfly vs. Iron Condor selection rule (volatility skew basis):**
  - If OTM puts trade at MUCH higher IV than OTM calls (steep skew): the Iron Butterfly's ATM put leg is fairly priced; OTM put wing protection is relatively cheap. **Prefer Iron Butterfly.**
  - If IV skew is relatively FLAT (OTM puts not much richer than calls): Iron Condor's OTM short legs capture more of the fairly-priced premium while keeping the profit zone wide. **Prefer Iron Condor.**
  - Shorthand: **Steep skew → Iron Butterfly; Flat skew → Iron Condor.**
- **Source:** *Trading Option Greeks* (Passarelli — Wing Spreads chapter on skew impact on butterfly selection); NSE Bank Nifty Options Strategies booklet.

### F79. "Selling the Guts" — Income Butterfly Concept (Passarelli)
- **Concept:** In an income-focused butterfly or condor, the profit comes from "selling the guts" — the middle strikes (the ATM or near-ATM short positions). The "wings" (the outer long positions) are purchased purely for protection; they have no income purpose.
- **Implication of volatility skew:** If the IV of the middle "guts" strikes (ATM) is high relative to the outer "wing" strikes, the income-generating portion of the spread is richer. The income trader should monitor whether:
  - The "guts" premium is HIGH (high IV at ATM) → favorable butterfly/condor entry (more income relative to wing cost).
  - The "guts" premium is LOW (depressed ATM IV) → unfavorable butterfly entry (little income; wings are expensive relative to guts).
- **Risk-reward impact:** When guts are "rich," max profit increases and risk-reward improves. When guts are "thin," max profit decreases and the trade may have negative EV even with high POP.
- **Source:** *Trading Option Greeks* (Passarelli — volatility skew and wing spreads chapter); extracted from the Q&A: "How does the 'richness' of the middle strikes change the risk-to-reward ratio?"

### F80. GEX-Based Condor Wing Sizing Rule (1 SD Placement)
- **Inputs/Calculation:**
  - In a positive-GEX environment (range-bound), Iron Condor wings should be placed approximately at the 1 SD expected move boundary from ATM.
  - Using the VIX formula (F9 in main KB): `1_SD_Move = Spot × (VIX/100) ÷ √252` (daily) or ÷ √52 (weekly).
  - For a 5–7 DTE iron condor: use the 5–7 day expected move (multiply daily move by √5 or √7).
  - The source material's standard is "~200 points OTM" for Nifty condor wings (from the strike selection table, F55 in Supplement Part 1) — verify this aligns with 1 SD at current VIX.
- **Check:** At VIX = 14, daily move ≈ 24,000 × 0.14 / 15.87 ≈ 212 pts. 5-day move ≈ 212 × √5 ≈ 474 pts. For a 7-DTE condor, placing wings 200 pts OTM means the short strikes are within the 5-day 1 SD band — consistent with the 55–65% POP claim in the source. At VIX = 20, the same 200-pt wings would be INSIDE the 1 SD move → much lower POP. **Adjust wing placement dynamically based on current VIX, not a fixed 200 pts.**
- **Rule:** `Optimal_Wing_Distance = 1_SD_Weekly_Move × 0.80` (place wings 80% of the 1 SD move away — slightly inside 1 SD to maximize premium while maintaining ~60% POP).

### F81. 0DTE Maximum 2 Trades Rule with Rationale
- **Rule:** On expiry day (0DTE), execute a maximum of 2 trades total. If both trades are losing trades, stop ALL trading for the remainder of that expiry session.
- **Rationale (from source):** Options on expiry day have maximum Gamma — small Nifty moves create extreme, fast option value swings in either direction. This means:
  1. Winning trades can produce very large % gains quickly (50–100% of premium in 15–60 minutes).
  2. Losing trades can destroy premium very quickly (a 30% stop-loss can be triggered within minutes).
  3. On a "bad" expiry day (e.g., Nifty trends strongly one way then the other), the Gamma environment means TWO losing trades can collectively wipe out a significant portion of weekly capital (2 × 30% stop-loss on the position = net 60% of premium lost plus transaction costs).
  4. After 2 losses on expiry day, continuing to trade is statistically likely to be in a negative-EV regime (the market is moving in ways your setup did not predict → remain flat, preserve capital).
- **Source:** nifty-options-pro `strategies_and_setups.md` (Expiry Day 0DTE Protocol: "Maximum 2 trades on expiry day. If both fail, stop.").

### F83. Long Box / Short Box — Arbitrage Position Mechanics
**From NSE Bank Nifty Options Strategies booklet (Neutral Strategies table):**
- **Long Box (Conversion):**
  - Buy Call A + Sell Call B + Buy Put B + Sell Put A (where B > A — higher strike)
  - Risk: NONE from price changes (the position is completely Delta-neutral and all Greek risks cancel)
  - Reward: FIXED — the difference between strike prices minus the net premium paid, regardless of where price is at expiry
  - Use case: Pure interest-rate/time-value arbitrage. Used when the present value of the fixed payoff exceeds the net cost to enter.
- **Short Box (Reversal):** Mirror image — Buy Call B + Sell Call A + Buy Put A + Sell Put B.
- **Why retail traders should know this:** When you see options pricing that implies a "box" return significantly different from the risk-free rate, it signals a pricing error somewhere in the option chain. These errors are instantly arbitraged away by market makers, which is why they're extremely rare in liquid markets like NIFTY.
- **Source:** NSE Bank Nifty Options Strategies booklet (Neutral Strategies table — Long Box and Short Box entries).

### F86. Ratio Spread P&L Profile (Kaushik Extended)
**From *Options Trading Handbook* (Kaushik) — 1:2 Call Ratio Spread:**
- **Construction:** Buy 1 ITM Call (lower strike) + Sell 2 OTM Calls (higher strike), same expiry.
- `Net_Credit = 2 × OTM_Call_Premium − ITM_Call_Premium` (ideally net credit or near-zero)
- **P&L profile:**
  - Below ITM strike at expiry: lose the net debit (or gain the net credit if received)
  - Between ITM and OTM strikes: profit increases as price rises (similar to a long call)
  - Exactly at OTM strike at expiry: maximum profit = `(OTM_Strike − ITM_Strike + Net_Credit) × Lot_Size`
  - ABOVE OTM strike: LOSS accelerates — the uncovered short call (the 2nd of the 2 short calls) creates an uncovered short → losses are theoretically unlimited above the OTM strike
- **Worked example (NIFTY at 9105.30, from Kaushik):** Buy 9100 CE + Sell 2× 9400 CE. Maximum profit if NIFTY closes at 9400 at expiry = (300 pts spread + net credit) × 25. If NIFTY closes above 9400 → losses begin accelerating with no cap.
- **Risk management critical rule:** Must monitor continuously and close intraday (Kaushik recommends closing by end of session). Never hold a 1:2 call ratio spread overnight in a trending bullish environment.
- **Source:** *Options Trading Handbook* (Kaushik); E18 in main KB (extended here with the full P&L profile that was missing).

### IR57. Gamma-Theta Tradeoff — Strategy Selection Rule
- **IF** you want positive Gamma (accelerating profits if market moves strongly in your favor)
- **THEN** you MUST accept negative Theta (you will lose money every day the market does NOT move). There is no way around this.
- **IF** you want positive Theta (collecting time decay as income)
- **THEN** you MUST accept negative Gamma (a large adverse move will hurt you acceleratingly, and you cannot benefit from Gamma acceleration). There is no way around this.
- **Application to regime matching:** In a SIDEWAYS market (positive GEX + compressed VIX), the Theta-positive / Gamma-negative position is STRUCTURALLY FAVORED because large moves are suppressed by dealer behavior. In a VOLATILE market (negative GEX + VIX Weak Low), the Gamma-positive / Theta-negative position is STRUCTURALLY FAVORED because a large move is exactly what is expected. Choosing the wrong side of the Gamma-Theta tradeoff for the current regime is the most common structural error in options trading.
- **CONFIDENCE: HIGH** — mathematical fact; consistently stated across all sources.
- **Source:** nifty-options-pro SKILL.md; *Trading Option Greeks* (Passarelli); P39 in this Part 2.

### IR58. IV vs. HV Trading Signal Rule
- **IF** Implied Volatility significantly exceeds Historical Volatility (IV >> HV, approximately IV/HV > 1.30)
- **THEN** options are overpriced relative to realized movement → prefer SELLING premium strategies (straddle, condor, strangle). The seller has a structural mathematical edge; the buyer is overpaying for expected movement that the underlying hasn't historically delivered.
- **IF** Implied Volatility approximately equals Historical Volatility (IV/HV between 0.90 and 1.10)
- **THEN** options are fairly priced → neutral; no structural edge for buyer or seller. Calendar spreads and defined-risk directional spreads are appropriate (they don't rely heavily on IV mispricing).
- **IF** Implied Volatility is below Historical Volatility (IV < HV, approximately IV/HV < 0.90)
- **THEN** options are underpriced → prefer BUYING premium or buying directional options. The buyer has the mathematical edge; the underlying has been moving MORE than the market expects, and this will be reflected in future realized vol.
- **⚠ Pairing rule:** This signal must ALWAYS be combined with GEX regime (IR56/P41). IV << HV (cheap options) in a NEGATIVE GEX regime = the maximum sweet spot for option buyers (structure favors large moves AND options are cheap). IV >> HV in a POSITIVE GEX regime = the maximum sweet spot for option sellers (structure dampens moves AND options are expensive).
- **CONFIDENCE: HIGH** for the principle; **MEDIUM** for the specific thresholds (1.30 and 0.90 are standard market-practitioner conventions, not derived from NIFTY-specific backtesting).
- **Source:** nifty-options-pro `greeks_and_pricing.md` ("IV vs HV edge" — verbatim: "When IV >> HV, options are expensive → prefer selling volatility. When IV ≈ HV or IV < HV, options fairly/cheaply priced → prefer buying or neutral strategies."); Natenberg; P40 in this Part 2.

Going further OTM (Delta < 0.20) reduces cost but also reduces to <20% probability of profiting from the option alone at expiry (though intraday moves can still create quick gains on OTM options before expiry).

Instead, consider buying the weekly for directional exposure (cheap risk, high potential reward if the event causes a large move) OR wait until AFTER the event to sell the weekly (post-event IV crush makes selling attractive).
- **IF** IV Rank is LOW (<30) for the current weekly expiry
- **THEN** buying directional options or straddles/strangles is structurally favored. Do NOT sell premium on a low-IV-Rank week (you're selling cheap) unless GEX is strongly positive (structural pin support for the range trade).
- **CONFIDENCE: HIGH** for the logic; **MEDIUM** for the specific IV Rank thresholds of 50 and 30 (standard market-practitioner conventions).
- **Source:** nifty-options-pro SKILL.md (expiry selection logic in F56, Part 1 Supplement); P44 in this Part 2.

### IR62. Iron Butterfly vs. Iron Condor Selection Rule (Skew-Based)
- **IF** the vertical IV skew is STEEP (OTM puts are trading at significantly higher IV than equidistant OTM calls — typically more than 3–5 IV points difference)
- **THEN** prefer an Iron Butterfly over an Iron Condor. The steep skew makes OTM put wings relatively cheap (you're buying protection at elevated IV but it's structurally justified by skew premium); meanwhile, the ATM short legs in the Iron Butterfly benefit from the higher ATM IV caused by the steep skew environment.
- **IF** the vertical IV skew is FLAT (OTM puts and OTM calls trading at similar IV levels)
- **THEN** prefer an Iron Condor over an Iron Butterfly. In a flat-skew environment, the wider profit zone of the condor (two OTM short legs vs. one ATM short leg) captures more premium without the concentrated risk of the ATM butterfly body.
- **CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM** — derived from Passarelli's wing-spread/skew analysis; the 3–5 IV point threshold for "steep" is an operational interpretation, not stated explicitly in the source.
- **Source:** *Trading Option Greeks* (Passarelli — Wing Spreads chapter); F78 in this Part 2.

### IR63. 0DTE Maximum 2 Trades Rule — Formal Entry
- **IF** you have taken 2 trades on an expiry Thursday AND both have hit their stop-losses
- **THEN** immediately stop ALL trading for the remainder of that session. No re-entry, no "averaging" the loss, no revenge trades.
- **IF** you have taken 2 trades on an expiry Thursday AND at least 1 has been profitable
- **THEN** you may consider ONE additional trade only if the 5-signal detection score (F16, main KB) has significantly changed since your last entry (new information has arrived — GEX has moved, VIX has changed structure). If the regime is the same, no new entry.
- **Rationale:** On a 2-loss expiry day, you are operating in a market that is NOT behaving as your model predicted. The correct response to a model failure is to STOP, not to increase exposure. The Gamma environment on expiry day means each additional trade after 2 losses has an even higher probability of further loss (you are now trading without a clear regime read).
- **CONFIDENCE: HIGH** — "Maximum 2 trades on expiry day. If both fail, stop." is verbatim from `strategies_and_setups.md`. The conditional rule for after 1 profitable + 1 loss trade is a logical extension, not explicitly stated.
- **Source:** nifty-options-pro `strategies_and_setups.md` (0DTE Protocol); F81 in this Part 2.

### IR64. OI 4-Combination Reading Rules (Formal IF-THEN for All 4)
- **IF** OI is Rising AND LTP is Rising at a specific strike** → THEN new long positions opening at that strike. Bulls are paying up; they expect price to move in their favor. Moderately bullish signal for that specific level.
- **IF** OI is Rising AND LTP is Falling at a specific strike** → THEN new short positions opening (writers). Bears are actively selling premium at this level. Bearish signal (IR1 in main KB — extending here with the comparative context).
- **IF** OI is Falling AND LTP is Rising at a specific strike** → THEN short positions are being closed (short covering). Previous writers are buying back their positions at a loss (or accepting profit). This is FORCED buying → can cause sharp upward price acceleration because shorts have no choice but to cover (see P43 in this Part 2). Often the most explosive bullish signal of all four.
- **IF** OI is Falling AND LTP is Falling at a specific strike** → THEN long positions are being closed (long liquidation). Previous buyers are exiting. Bearish signal — less explosive than short covering, but still directionally meaningful.
- **CONFIDENCE: HIGH** for all four interpretations — these are mechanically derived from the definitions of OI and premium direction; confirmed across multiple sources.
- **Source:** nifty-options-pro `market_structure_gex.md`; *Trading Option Greeks* (Passarelli); P38 in this Part 2.

### IR65. Frictionless Market Corrections for Real-World NIFTY Trading
- **IF** using a theoretical option price from Black-Scholes or Monte Carlo as a reference point for entry/exit decisions
- **THEN** apply these four real-world corrections BEFORE acting on the theoretical price:
  1. **Subtract transaction costs:** ~₹85–95/lot round trip (F35 in main KB). A theoretical edge of ₹50/lot is actually a negative-EV trade after costs.
  2. **Account for bid-ask spread:** The theoretical mid-price of an option is rarely available at execution. Entry is at the ask (above mid); exit is at the bid (below mid). If the bid-ask spread is ₹5, your effective entry is ₹2.50 above theoretical and exit is ₹2.50 below — total slippage of ₹5 per lot.
  3. **Discretize adjustments:** The model assumes continuous hedging; in practice, you adjust Delta at discrete intervals (2:00 PM daily, or at 50-pt move triggers). This discretization introduces tracking error vs. the theoretical hedge.
  4. **Circuit breaker risk:** In extreme cascade events, trading may be halted for 15–45 minutes. During a halt, you CANNOT adjust your delta-hedge. If you are short Gamma (naked straddle, condor) during a halt, you have unlimited loss exposure with no ability to manage it until trading resumes.
- **CONFIDENCE: HIGH** for the principle (Natenberg explicitly names the "frictionless illusion" as one of three core lessons); **MEDIUM** for the specific slippage and tracking-error numbers (these are operational estimates, not from backtesting).
- **Source:** Natenberg — "Lesson 3: The Frictionless Illusion"; F35 and F85 in this KB.

### IR66. Ratio Spread — Session Timing and Monitoring Rule
- **IF** entering a call ratio spread (buy 1 ITM call + sell 2 OTM calls) or put ratio spread
- **THEN** apply these rules:
  1. **Intraday only:** Close the position before end of session (do not hold overnight). The uncovered second short call creates unbounded overnight gap-risk.
  2. **Monitor the underlying's approach to the short strike:** If the underlying approaches the OTM short strike during the session, close the position immediately — do not wait for end-of-day. The P&L profile deteriorates rapidly above/below the short strike.
  3. **No ratio spreads near major events:** The unbounded loss on the excess short leg makes ratio spreads particularly dangerous when a large move is possible (earnings, RBI policy, etc.).
- **CONFIDENCE: HIGH** for Rule 1 (explicitly recommended by Kaushik: "finalize trades by end of trading day"); **MEDIUM** for Rules 2 and 3 (logical extensions not explicitly stated in the source).
- **Source:** *Options Trading Handbook* (Kaushik); E18 in main KB.

### IR67. Lognormal vs. Normal Distribution — Operational Implications for NIFTY
- **IF** using the NIFTY expected move formulas (F9 in main KB) for setting strike placement or trade targets
- **THEN** be aware of two operational implications of the lognormal distribution:
  1. **Downside moves require LESS movement to reach a given strike than upside moves.** If Nifty is at 24,000 and a 1 SD daily move is 200 pts, moving DOWN 200 pts to 23,800 is a 0.833% move in the underlying; moving UP 200 pts to 24,200 is also 0.833%. But in lognormal terms, the DOWN move contracts the denominator (23,800 is 0.833% less), while the UP move expands it (24,200 is 0.833% more). In practice at NIFTY's scale this effect is negligible within 1 SD, but becomes meaningful for 2–3 SD moves.
  2. **OTM call options are inherently worth slightly more than equidistant OTM puts** (before the skew effect — the institutional demand for puts reverses this in practice). If your model is pricing OTM calls equal to OTM puts, the model is understating call value.
- **CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM** — both implications are derived from Natenberg's lognormal discussion; the practical magnitude at NIFTY's typical ranges is small (the skew effect from institutional demand is larger and in the OPPOSITE direction for index options). Include for completeness; calibrate importance vs. skew.
- **Source:** Natenberg — "Phase 1: Mathematics of Price Distribution"; P34 in Supplement Part 1.

### IR68. Collar Strategy — When to Use vs. Outright Put Buy
- **IF** you hold a long position in NIFTY (or NIFTY BeES ETF) and want downside protection
- **THEN** compare:
  - **Buy protective put only:** Costs full put premium. Upside is fully open. Best when you want maximum upside participation and can afford the full premium cost.
  - **Collar (buy put + sell call):** Net cost is near-zero (call premium received partially/fully offsets put premium paid). Downside protected. BUT upside is capped at the short call strike. Best when you want cheap/free protection and are willing to give up some upside potential.
- **Collar decision rule:** If the call premium (at your willing upside cap level) ≥ put premium (at your needed downside protection level) → zero-cost collar is available. Enter the collar. If call premium < put premium → collar has a net cost; decide based on how much downside protection is worth vs. the upside cap you accept.
- **Source:** NSE Bank Nifty Options Strategies booklet (Collar strategy entry in Bullish Strategies table); Thomsett ("Dividend Collar" referenced in ToC); Kaushik (covered call + put protection context).

### IR69. Maximum Profit Should Not Be The Goal on Short Premium Trades
- **IF** a short premium trade (straddle, condor, strangle, credit spread) has reached 70–80% of its theoretical maximum profit
- **THEN** close the position NOW. Do NOT wait for the "last rupee."
- **Rationale:** The last 20–30% of a short premium trade's maximum profit requires holding through the most dangerous Gamma period (the final 1–3 days before expiry, when Gamma is at its maximum). The risk-reward of holding for that last 20–30% is extremely unfavorable — a single large move can turn a near-maximum-profit position into a significant loss within hours.
- **Mathematical statement:** The expected value of holding a short condor from 80% profit to 100% profit is NEGATIVE once the transaction costs of closing later (vs. now) and the Gamma risk of holding are accounted for.
- **CONFIDENCE: HIGH** — stated as an explicit rule in `risk_and_adjustments.md`: "Exit at profit target: 70–80% of maximum profit (don't wait for last rupee)."
- **Source:** nifty-options-pro `risk_and_adjustments.md` (Exit Rules, also repeated in F58 Rule 1 of Supplement Part 1 — added as a formal IR rule here since the rationale makes it more actionable).

### IR70. Synthetic Position Use — Specific Decision Rules
- **IF** a call option is overpriced relative to theoretical value AND a put at the same strike is fairly priced
- **THEN** construct a Synthetic Long Put instead: Short Stock + Long Call. This gives you the same payoff as an overpriced put but at a lower cost, because you're buying the fairly-priced call instead.
- **IF** a put option is overpriced relative to theoretical value AND a call at the same strike is fairly priced
- **THEN** construct a Synthetic Long Call: Long Stock + Long Put. Buy the fairly-priced put instead of the overpriced call.
- **General principle:** Whenever one leg of put-call parity is trading at a significant premium to theoretical value, the arbitrage-free equivalent synthetic position (using the fairly-priced leg + underlying) is more capital-efficient.
- **⚠ Practical limit:** These synthetics require ability to buy/sell the underlying stock or futures, which adds capital and margin requirements. For retail AXIS users, the synthetic route is most practical using NIFTY futures (which have defined margin requirements) as the "stock" equivalent.
- **CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM** — logically derived from Passarelli (Put-Call Parity and Synthetics chapter); no specific NIFTY dated examples in the source material.
- **Source:** *Trading Option Greeks* (Passarelli); P46 in this Part 2.

### IR71. "Selling the Guts" Assessment Rule
- **IF** considering a butterfly or condor entry
- **THEN** first assess the "guts" premium (the ATM or near-ATM short leg IV) relative to the "wings" (the OTM long leg IV):
  1. If the IV at the short-strike guts is HIGH relative to the wing strikes → the trade has favorable income-to-protection cost ratio. Proceed.
  2. If the IV at the short-strike guts is COMPRESSED (low) while the wings are relatively expensive → the maximum profit on the butterfly/condor is small while the downside cost is large. Avoid or wait for better IV conditions at ATM.
- **Practical check:** If IV at ATM > IV at wings (which occurs when IV skew is "inverted" — ATM more expensive than OTM), the butterfly is very attractive to sell. This is a rare regime but occurs occasionally around near-term events where near-dated ATM IV spikes.
- **CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM** — derived from Passarelli's "selling the guts" concept; specific IV comparison rule is an AXIS interpretation of the principle, not numerically defined in the source.
- **Source:** *Trading Option Greeks* (Passarelli — "Selling the Guts" Q&A response); F79 in this Part 2.

### IR72. GEX-Based Wing Sizing Override
- **IF** placing Iron Condor wings using the standard "200 pts OTM" rule (from Strike Selection Guidelines, F55 in Supplement Part 1)
- **THEN** first verify that 200 pts is at least 80% of the current VIX-based weekly expected move (from F9 in main KB and F80 in this Part 2):
  - Calculate weekly expected move: `Weekly_Move = Spot × (VIX/100) ÷ √52`
  - Calculate 80% level: `Optimal_Wing = Weekly_Move × 0.80`
  - If Optimal_Wing < 200 pts → use Optimal_Wing (market is quiet; 200 pts is TOO FAR OTM; premium will be too thin)
  - If Optimal_Wing > 200 pts → use Optimal_Wing (market is volatile; 200 pts is TOO CLOSE TO ATM; POP will be below 55%)
- **Dynamic sizing example:** VIX = 10, Spot = 24,000 → Weekly_Move = 24,000 × 0.10 ÷ 7.21 ≈ 333 pts. Optimal_Wing = 333 × 0.80 ≈ 266 pts. Use 266 pts OTM (not 200). VIX = 20 → Weekly_Move ≈ 666 pts. Optimal_Wing ≈ 533 pts. Use 533 pts OTM.
- **CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM** for the formula (the 80% factor is a standard practitioner convention for balancing POP vs. premium collection); the VIX-wing relationship is derived from first principles, not stated in this exact form in the source.
- **Source:** nifty-options-pro `strategies_and_setups.md` (200 pts rule); F80 in this Part 2; F9 in main KB.

### IR73. Short Squeeze / Cascade Acceleration Recognition Rule
- **IF** price has risen through (or fallen through) a level where a large number of stop-losses were known to be clustered (e.g., above equal highs, above a round number, above a major swing high)
- **AND** the move accelerates dramatically after breaching that level (each tick seems to spawn 2 more ticks in the same direction)
- **THEN** recognize this as a stop-loss cascade (short squeeze if going up, long liquidation cascade if going down). The acceleration is MECHANICAL, not fundamental:
  - Do NOT buy the continuation momentum expecting it to persist — the fuel (stop orders) is being exhausted with every tic.
  - DO monitor for the moment the acceleration visibly slows (footprint: volume thins at new highs/lows = P9 Exhaustion step, main KB) → that is the entry for a counter-trade.
  - If it is expiry day and GEX is negative: these cascades can be much larger and persist longer before exhausting (dealer delta-hedge selling ADDS to the cascade rather than dampening it).
- **CONFIDENCE: HIGH** for the identification pattern; **MEDIUM** for the specific counter-trade timing (requires footprint confirmation, which adds a Layer C tool to the setup).
- **Source:** Wyckoff 2.0 (Short Squeeze mechanics); P43 in this Part 2; P4 (Liquidity Sweep) in main KB.

### IR74. Kaushik ETF Covered Call — Portfolio Adjustment After Assignment
- **IF** you are running the Money Tree covered call strategy (F63 in Supplement Part 1) AND the call is exercised (NIFTY BeES/BankNifty BeES units are called away at the strike)
- **THEN** immediately apply this decision process:
  1. Calculate remaining ETF units after delivery.
  2. If remaining units < 7,500: DO NOT write new covered calls. Resume SIP contributions (weekly ₹5,000 or chosen amount) until units are restored to ≥7,500.
  3. If remaining units ≥ 7,500: Write new covered calls at 5% above current market price immediately to restart income generation.
  4. Reinvest all received premium (from the delivered units at call strike + any excess SIP funds) into additional ETF units at current market price (dollar-cost-averaging into the dip created by the call exercise).
- **Rationale:** The call exercise is NOT a loss — you sold units at the call strike (which was 5% above your original price when you sold the call); you've captured a +5% gain on those units plus the premium received. Reinvesting at current prices (which may be above or below the call strike) continues the compounding cycle.
- **CONFIDENCE: HIGH** for the framework (directly from Kaushik); **MEDIUM** for the specific 7,500 minimum and the immediate-reinvestment rule (stated in Kaushik; operational details of timing may vary).
- **Source:** *Options Trading Handbook* (Kaushik — "Overseeing and Adjusting the Financial Portfolio" section).

### IR75. Neutral Market Signal — When to Use Range vs. Volatility Strategy
- **IF** the market regime is NEUTRAL (Direction Scorer output = 3, AND GEX is positive, AND VIX is compressed and stable)
- **THEN** this is NOT the same as "no trade." A neutral score in a positive-GEX / compressed-VIX environment is an explicit RANGE TRADE signal:
  - Deploy: Iron Condor (sell OTM calls AND OTM puts) or ATM Straddle sale.
  - These strategies PROFIT from a neutral / range-bound market — the neutral score IS the directional signal for these strategies.
- **IF** the market regime is NEUTRAL (Direction Scorer = 3) AND GEX is NEGATIVE AND VIX is at a Weak Low
- **THEN** this is a WAIT signal, NOT a range-trade signal. The negative GEX means a large move is possible at any time; selling premium in this environment is dangerous even if no directional signal exists yet. Wait for GEX to confirm direction before acting.
- **CONFIDENCE: HIGH** for the two distinct neutral interpretations; the regime-dependent difference in what "neutral" means is consistently supported across the GEX × VIX strategy matrix (F54, Supplement Part 1) and the signal hierarchy (P41 in this Part 2).
- **Source:** nifty-options-pro SKILL.md; `market_structure_gex.md`; F54 in Supplement Part 1.
